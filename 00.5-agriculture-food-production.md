# Chapter 0.5: Agriculture and Food Production (Years 1-10)

## Why This Chapter Comes First

You've survived the immediate crisis. You have water, basic shelter, and sanitation (Chapter -1). Now you face the harder question: how do you create enough food surplus to free people from constant subsistence farming so they can specialize in metallurgy, chemistry, and eventually computing?

**The brutal truth: You cannot rebuild technology without agricultural surplus.**

A person farming for pure subsistence has no time to smith metal, experiment with chemistry, or build radios. Historical civilizations only developed technology once agriculture became efficient enough that 90% of people weren't required to produce food. You must achieve this same transition.

This chapter will take you from Year 1 subsistence (everyone farms) to Year 10 surplus (specialized craftspeople possible).

---

## 1. The Foundation: Why Agriculture Matters

### The Specialization Problem

**Year 1 Reality:**
- One person farming poorly can barely feed themselves
- 10 people farming poorly can barely feed 10 people
- No one has time for anything but food production
- Technology progress: Zero

**Year 5 Goal:**
- 10 people farming well can feed 15 people
- 5 people freed for specialization (metalwork, salvage, tool-making)
- Technology progress: Basic metallurgy, tool improvement

**Year 10 Goal:**
- 10 people farming efficiently can feed 25-30 people
- 15-20 people freed for specialization
- Technology progress: Electricity, basic chemistry, advanced metalwork

### Productivity Curve

**Without improved agriculture:**
- 1 farmer feeds 1.2 people (starvation risk)
- Zero technological progress possible

**With improved agriculture:**
- 1 farmer feeds 3-5 people (surplus)
- Metallurgists, chemists, machinists, teachers become possible

**Historical reference:** Pre-industrial European farmers fed ~3-4 people each. This enabled blacksmiths, millers, scholars, and craftsmen. This is your target.

## 2. Seed Saving and Preservation

### Why Genetic Diversity Is Critical

Modern agriculture relies on hybrid seeds that don't breed true. Heirloom and open-pollinated varieties are essential for long-term survival. One crop disease can wipe out a genetically uniform population. Diversity is insurance.

### Seed Identification and Salvage

**Priority Salvage Locations:**
- Home gardens (often heirloom varieties)
- Seed companies and garden centers (grab everything)
- Agricultural research stations (diverse genetics)
- Grain elevators (wheat, corn, soybeans—may be hybrid but still valuable)
- Root cellars and pantries (dried beans, peas)

**Seed Viability Testing:**

Before relying on salvaged seeds, test viability:

1. Count out 10 seeds
2. Place between damp paper towels
3. Keep warm (70°F) and moist
4. Check after 7-14 days
5. If 8+ germinate: excellent (80%+ viability)
6. If 5-7 germinate: acceptable (50-70% viability)
7. If fewer than 5: poor viability, plant heavily or find better seeds

**Seed Lifespan (Properly Stored):**
- Onions, parsnips, parsley: 1-2 years
- Corn, peas, beans: 2-3 years
- Lettuce, peppers, carrots: 3-4 years
- Cabbage, radishes, turnips: 4-5 years
- Tomatoes, squash, cucumbers: 5-6 years
- Note: These are maximums. Viability decreases over time. Test yearly.

### Proper Seed Storage

**Enemies of Seed Viability:**
1. Heat (accelerates aging)
2. Moisture (causes mold and premature sprouting)
3. Light (triggers biochemical degradation)
4. Pests (mice, insects)

**Storage Protocol:**

1. **Drying:** Seeds must be bone-dry before storage
   - Spread seeds on screen or cloth
   - Dry in warm, well-ventilated area (not in direct sun)
   - Drying time: 1-2 weeks
   - Test: Bite a seed. Should crack cleanly, not bend.

2. **Containers:**
   - Glass jars with tight lids (ideal)
   - Metal tins (good)
   - Paper envelopes inside sealed containers (acceptable)
   - Plastic bags (least ideal but better than nothing)

3. **Desiccant:** Include moisture absorber
   - Salvaged silica gel packets (regenerate by heating)
   - Powdered milk (2 tablespoons wrapped in cloth per jar)
   - Rice (less effective but available)

4. **Storage Location:**
   - Cool: 40°F or cooler (root cellar, underground)
   - Dark: No light exposure
   - Dry: Low humidity
   - Mouse-proof: Metal containers or elevated storage

5. **Labeling:** Critical!
   ```
   Seed: Detroit Dark Red Beet
   Source: 123 Main Street garden
   Year Harvested: 2027
   Notes: Disease-resistant, good storage
   ```

### Seed Saving Techniques

**Open-Pollinated vs. Hybrid:**
- Open-pollinated: Seeds breed true. Save these.
- Hybrid (F1): Second generation won't match parent. Use but don't rely on long-term.
- GMO: May not germinate or breed true. Use first year, replace with saved seeds.

**Basic Seed Saving Procedure:**

**Dry Seeds (Beans, Peas, Grains):**
1. Let pods/heads dry completely on plant
2. Harvest when brittle
3. Shell or thresh to extract seeds
4. Winnow (separate seeds from chaff using wind)
5. Dry for additional 1-2 weeks
6. Store

**Wet Seeds (Tomatoes, Cucumbers, Squash):**
1. Select best fruit from healthiest plant
2. Let ripen fully (even overripe)
3. Extract seeds
4. For tomatoes: Ferment in water 2-3 days (kills diseases, removes gel)
5. Rinse thoroughly
6. Dry on screens
7. Store

**Biennials (Carrots, Beets, Cabbage, Onions):**
- These flower in second year
- Overwinter best plants
- Let flower and go to seed in Year 2
- Harvest seed heads when dry
- Thresh and store

**Isolation Distances (Prevent Cross-Pollination):**

Critical for maintaining variety purity:

- Corn: 400-1000 feet (wind-pollinated, cross-pollination very likely)
- Squash/pumpkins: 200-500 feet (insect-pollinated, different species can cross)
- Brassicas (cabbage, broccoli, kale): 200-400 feet
- Beans/peas: 10-20 feet (mostly self-pollinating)
- Tomatoes, peppers: 10-20 feet (mostly self-pollinating)
- Lettuce: 10 feet (self-pollinating)

**If space is limited:** Hand-pollinate and bag flowers, or accept that varieties may cross (often results are still useful).

### Building a Seed Bank

**Year 1 Minimum (Survival Seeds):**
- Beans (protein, nitrogen-fixing): 3+ varieties
- Peas (early protein): 2 varieties
- Potatoes (tubers, not seeds): 3+ varieties
- Corn (calories, stores well): 2+ varieties
- Squash (stores well, calories): 2+ varieties
- Cabbage family (stores well): 2+ varieties
- Root crops (carrots, beets, turnips): 3+ varieties
- Greens (lettuce, spinach): 2+ varieties
- Tomatoes (nutrition, morale): 2+ varieties

**Year 5 Expansion:**
- Grain crops (wheat, oats, barley, rye)
- Cooking crops (onions, garlic, herbs)
- Fibers (flax, cotton if climate suitable)
- Oil seeds (sunflowers, flax)

**Year 10 Advanced:**
- Specialized varieties for different uses
- Regional adaptations
- Trade with other communities for diversity

### Genetic Diversity Strategy

**Never rely on single varieties.** Plant at least 2-3 varieties of each crop:
- Different maturation times (stagger harvest, spread risk)
- Different disease resistances
- Different climate tolerances

Example bean strategy:
- Variety A: Early-season, cold-tolerant
- Variety B: Mid-season, drought-tolerant
- Variety C: Late-season, disease-resistant

If one fails, others may succeed.

## 3. Crop Selection for Rebuilding

### Criteria for Priority Crops

1. **Calories per acre:** High energy return
2. **Growth speed:** Fast return on labor
3. **Storage capability:** Lasts through winter
4. **Nutritional completeness:** Protein, carbs, vitamins
5. **Input requirements:** Low fertilizer/water needs
6. **Failure tolerance:** Can skip a year and still survive

### Tier 1 Crops: Plant These First

**Potatoes**

Why essential:
- Highest calories per acre of any crop (9,000,000 per acre vs 3,000,000 for grain)
- Fast growing (90-120 days)
- Stores 6-8 months in root cellar
- Easy to grow

Planting:
- Cut seed potatoes into pieces with 2-3 eyes each
- Let cut surfaces dry 24 hours (prevents rot)
- Plant 4 inches deep, 12 inches apart, rows 30 inches apart
- Hill soil around plants as they grow (protects tubers)

Harvest:
- Dig when foliage dies back
- Let skins cure (dry) 1-2 weeks before storage
- Store in dark, cool (38-40°F), high humidity

Problems:
- Late blight (destroyed Irish potato crop, can destroy yours)
- Solution: Multiple varieties, remove infected plants immediately

**Beans (Dry)**

Why essential:
- Protein (20-25% protein content)
- Nitrogen-fixing (improves soil for next crop)
- Stores 2+ years dry
- Multiple harvests from saved seed

Types:
- Bush beans: Faster, no support needed
- Pole beans: Higher yield, need supports

Planting:
- After last frost
- 1 inch deep, 3-4 inches apart
- Rows 18-24 inches apart

Harvest:
- Leave pods on plant until dry and brittle
- Shell beans from pods
- Dry further before storage

**Winter Squash/Pumpkins**

Why essential:
- High calories
- Stores 3-6 months without processing
- Productive (10-20 squash per plant)
- Seeds edible (protein, oil)

Planting:
- After frost danger
- Hills 4-6 feet apart
- 2-3 seeds per hill, thin to 1 plant

Harvest:
- When skin is hard (fingernail won't penetrate)
- Before frost
- Cure 10-14 days in warm, dry location
- Store in cool (50-55°F), dry location

**Cabbage Family (Cabbage, Kale, Broccoli)**

Why essential:
- Cabbage stores 3-4 months fresh
- Kale survives frost, provides greens through winter
- High nutrients
- Makes sauerkraut (fermented storage)

Planting:
- Cool season crop
- Start early spring or late summer
- Transplant seedlings 18-24 inches apart

Harvest:
- Cabbage: When heads firm
- Kale: Pick outer leaves, plant keeps producing
- Store cabbage in root cellar or ferment

**Grains (Wheat, Oats, Barley, Rye)**

Why essential:
- Calories (bread = civilization)
- Stores years if kept dry
- Relatively low labor once established
- Straw useful for animal bedding, mulch, thatching

Planting:
- Fall (winter wheat) or spring (spring wheat, oats, barley)
- Broadcast seed or drill in rows
- 100-120 lbs seed per acre (hand broadcast higher)

Harvest:
- When grain heads are dry and golden
- Cut with scythe or sickle
- Bundle into sheaves, let dry
- Thresh (separate grain from straw)
- Winnow (separate grain from chaff)

Storage:
- Must be completely dry (bite test: cracks, doesn't dent)
- Store in mouse-proof bins
- Check monthly for moisture/pests

**Corn**

Why essential:
- High calories
- Multi-use (fresh, dried, cornmeal, animal feed)
- Stores well as dry kernels
- Stalks useful as animal feed

Planting:
- After frost danger, soil 60°F+
- Blocks (multiple short rows) not single rows (better pollination)
- 8-12 inches apart, rows 30 inches apart

Harvest:
- Sweet corn: When kernels milky
- Dry corn: When kernels hard, husks brown
- Dry further after harvest

Note: Save your own seed or corn will revert to field corn (starchy, not sweet).

### Tier 2 Crops: Add When Tier 1 Stable

**Root Crops (Carrots, Beets, Turnips, Parsnips)**
- Storage crops (4-6 months in root cellar)
- Turnips/parsnips can overwinter in ground in mild climates
- Nutrient-dense

**Onions and Garlic**
- Essential flavoring (morale)
- Stores 6-8 months
- Antimicrobial properties (health)

**Tomatoes**
- Nutrition (Vitamin C)
- Morale (familiar taste)
- Can/preserve for winter

**Greens (Lettuce, Spinach, Chard)**
- Fast growing (30-60 days)
- Vitamins
- Fill gaps between main crops

**Cucurbits (Cucumbers, Melons)**
- Summer food
- Can pickle cucumbers (storage)
- Morale

### Crops to Avoid Early On

**Resource-Intensive:**
- Asparagus (3 years to production)
- Fruit trees (5-7 years to production)
- Berries (2-3 years to production)

**Low Return:**
- Celery (high water needs, low calories)
- Lettuce (low calories, doesn't store)

**Plant these once you have surplus capacity, not before.**

### Calculating Food Needs

**Per Person Annual Requirements:**
- 2000 calories per day = 730,000 calories per year
- Safety margin: Plan for 900,000 calories (accounts for waste, bad years)

**Crop Yields (Per Acre, Good Management):**
- Potatoes: 10,000 lbs = 9 million calories
- Corn: 150 bushels = 4.5 million calories
- Wheat: 40 bushels = 2.4 million calories
- Beans: 1500 lbs = 2.5 million calories
- Squash: 20,000 lbs = 1.8 million calories

**Example 10-Person Community:**
- Need: 9 million calories
- 1 acre potatoes = 9 million calories (meets need)
- But never mono-crop: Risk of total loss

**Diverse Plan:**
- 0.5 acre potatoes: 4.5 million calories
- 0.5 acre beans: 1.25 million calories
- 0.5 acre corn: 2.25 million calories
- 0.5 acre squash: 900,000 calories
- 0.5 acre grains: 1.2 million calories
- 0.5 acre other (greens, roots, etc): 500,000 calories
- **Total: 10.6 million calories (sufficient with margin)**

**Labor calculation:**
- 3 acres well-managed = 1-2 people full-time + seasonal help
- Feeds 10 people
- Frees 8 people for other work

## 3.5. Climate Adaptation and Nutritional Completeness

### Climate Variation: Adapt to Your Region

The crop recommendations above assume temperate growing conditions (similar to central/northern United States or Europe). **Your climate determines your strategy.**

**Arid/Desert Regions (American Southwest, Mediterranean, Middle East):**
- **Priority**: Water above all else
- **Irrigation first, expansion second**: Don't plant more than you can water
- **Crops**: Tepary beans (drought-tolerant), amaranth, sorghum, dates, olives
- **Strategies**:
  - Deep watering, less frequent (encourages deep roots)
  - Mulch heavily (reduces evaporation)
  - Plant in depressions to catch water
  - Consider night planting (less water loss)
- **Timeline**: Expect slower agricultural expansion; irrigation infrastructure is prerequisite

**Tropical/Humid Regions (Coastal Florida, Southeast Asia, Central America):**
- **Challenge**: Year-round growing but also year-round pests/disease
- **Crops**: Cassava (manioc), taro, sweet potato, plantains, rice (if water available)
- **Strategies**:
  - Crop rotation critical (disease pressure high)
  - Elevated beds (drainage in heavy rain)
  - Multiple small plantings (hedge against disease)
  - Preserve aggressively (food spoils faster in heat/humidity)
- **Timeline**: Can achieve surplus faster (multiple crops/year) but disease management harder

**Cold/Short Growing Season (Northern Canada, Scandinavia, High Altitude):**
- **Challenge**: 90-120 day growing season or less
- **Crops**: Cold-hardy varieties essential
  - Fast-maturing potatoes (60-90 day varieties)
  - Short-season grains (barley, oats, spring wheat)
  - Root vegetables (turnips, rutabagas, carrots - can handle frost)
  - Hardy greens (kale, collards - survive light frost)
- **Strategies**:
  - Season extension (cold frames, hoop houses, row covers)
  - Start seeds indoors (salvage windows, build greenhouse)
  - Focus on storage crops (must last 8-9 month winter)
  - Preserve/ferment during short harvest window
- **Timeline**: Expect longer to reach surplus; each failed season is a full year lost

**Coastal Regions:**
- **Advantages**: Moderate temperatures, fishing supplements food, salt readily available
- **Challenges**: Salt spray, wind, sandy soil, storm damage
- **Crops**: Salt-tolerant varieties, fruit trees (wind protection), root vegetables
- **Strategies**:
  - Windbreaks essential (salvaged fence, planted trees)
  - Soil amendment critical (sandy soil needs organic matter)
  - Fish waste for fertilizer (high nitrogen)
  - Seaweed for trace minerals

**Adapt the general principles to your specific climate. No single formula works everywhere.**

### Nutritional Completeness: Beyond Calories

The calorie calculations above show energy per acre, but **a diet of only potatoes leads to malnutrition.** You need complete nutrition.

**Minimum Dietary Requirements (Per Person, Per Year):**

**Calories: 2,000-2,500/day minimum**
- High-labor farming: 3,000+/day needed
- Children, elderly: 1,500-2,000/day
- Source: Grains, potatoes, fats

**Protein: 50-60 grams/day minimum**
- Deficiency leads to: Muscle wasting, edema, weakened immune system, death
- Plant sources (grams protein per cup cooked):
  - Beans: 15g
  - Lentils: 18g
  - Peas: 9g
  - Quinoa: 8g
- Animal sources (per 100g):
  - Chicken: 27g
  - Fish: 20-25g
  - Eggs: 13g (per egg)
  - Milk: 3g (per cup)

**Critical Vitamins/Minerals:**

**Vitamin C (Scurvy Prevention):**
- Need: 10mg/day minimum to prevent scurvy, 75-90mg/day optimal
- Symptoms of deficiency: Bleeding gums, loose teeth, poor wound healing, death (3-6 months without)
- Sources:
  - Potatoes: 20mg per medium potato (cooking reduces this)
  - Cabbage: 30mg per cup
  - Peppers: 120mg per pepper
  - Rose hips: 2,000mg per cup (wild harvest)
- **Strategy**: Plant cabbage family, preserve sauerkraut (retains vitamin C), forage rose hips

**Vitamin A (Night Blindness, Immune Function):**
- Deficiency symptoms: Night blindness, dry eyes, frequent infections
- Sources:
  - Carrots: High
  - Sweet potatoes: Very high
  - Dark leafy greens: Moderate
  - Liver (animal): Extremely high

**B Vitamins (Pellagra, Beriberi Prevention):**
- Deficiency symptoms:
  - Niacin (B3): Pellagra - diarrhea, dermatitis, dementia, death
  - Thiamine (B1): Beriberi - nerve damage, heart failure
- Sources:
  - Whole grains (not white rice or white flour!)
  - Beans, peas, lentils
  - Meat, eggs
- **Critical**: Eat whole grains, not refined. Polished rice causes beriberi.

**Iron (Anemia Prevention):**
- Deficiency symptoms: Fatigue, weakness, pale skin, shortness of breath
- Sources:
  - Beans, lentils
  - Dark leafy greens (spinach, kale)
  - Meat (most bioavailable form)
- Note: Vitamin C aids iron absorption (eat greens with acidic foods)

**Calcium (Bone/Tooth Health):**
- Sources:
  - Dairy (milk, cheese)
  - Dark leafy greens (kale, collards)
  - Bones (boil, eat marrow, make bone broth)

### Minimum Diet Diversity (Weekly)

**To prevent deficiency diseases, each person needs per week:**

- **Protein source**: Beans/lentils (3-4 cups dry) OR meat/fish (500-700g) OR eggs (14+) OR combination
- **Vitamin C source**: Cabbage, peppers, or potatoes (daily if possible)
- **Vitamin A source**: Carrots, sweet potatoes, or dark greens (3+ times/week)
- **Whole grains**: Not refined (daily) - prevents beriberi/pellagra
- **Dietary fat**: Nuts, seeds, animal fat, or oil (prevents fat-soluble vitamin deficiency)

**Minimum Land for Nutritional Completeness (Per Person):**

- Calories (grains/potatoes): 0.1-0.15 acres
- Protein (beans or animals): 0.05-0.1 acres
- Vegetables (vitamins/minerals): 0.05 acres
- **Total: 0.2-0.3 acres per person minimum** (more with inefficiency, less with very intensive methods)

**For 50 people:**
- 10-15 acres minimum for nutritional completeness
- Requires crop diversity, not monoculture

### Recognizing Crop Diseases

**You must be able to identify common diseases early. One diseased field can destroy your community.**

**Late Blight (Potatoes, Tomatoes) - DEADLY**
- **Caused by**: Phytophthora infestans (water mold, not fungus)
- **Symptoms**:
  - Dark, water-soaked spots on leaves
  - White fungal growth on underside of leaves (in humid conditions)
  - Brown/black lesions on stems
  - Tubers develop brown rot
- **Progression**: Can destroy entire field in 7-14 days
- **Action**:
  - Remove infected plants IMMEDIATELY
  - Burn them (do NOT compost - spores survive)
  - Do not save seed from infected crop
  - Plant different varieties in different locations (don't lose all potatoes)
- **This killed a million people in Ireland. Take it seriously.**

**Rust (Wheat, Barley, Oats)**
- **Symptoms**: Orange/brown pustules on leaves and stems (looks like rust)
- **Action**: Remove infected plants, rotate crops, plant resistant varieties

**Powdery Mildew (Squash, Cucumbers, Many Crops)**
- **Symptoms**: White powdery coating on leaves
- **Action**: Improve air circulation, remove infected leaves, sulfur dust if available

**Mosaic Virus (Beans, Tomatoes, Cucumbers)**
- **Symptoms**: Yellow mottling/mosaic pattern on leaves, stunted growth
- **Spread by**: Aphids, contaminated tools
- **Action**: Remove infected plants, control aphids, clean tools between plants

**Bacterial Wilt (Cucumbers, Squash)**
- **Symptoms**: Rapid wilting despite adequate water, slimy stems when cut
- **Spread by**: Cucumber beetles
- **Action**: Remove infected plants, control beetles

**Simple Diagnostic Heuristic:**
- **Brown spots spreading**: Fungal disease (blight, rust) - remove plants, improve drainage
- **Yellow mottling/patterns**: Viral (mosaic, yellows) - remove plants, control insects
- **Wilting despite water**: Bacterial or root disease - remove plants, rotate location
- **White powder on leaves**: Powdery mildew - improve air flow, less overhead watering

**Disease Prevention Strategies:**
1. **Crop rotation**: Never plant same family in same spot consecutively
2. **Diversity**: Multiple varieties of each crop (one disease won't kill all)
3. **Spacing**: Good air circulation prevents fungal diseases
4. **Sanitation**: Remove infected plants immediately, burn them
5. **Tool cleaning**: Disease spreads on dirty tools - clean between plants
6. **Seed saving**: Never save seed from diseased plants

## 4. Soil Management and Crop Rotation

### Understanding Soil Health

Soil is not dirt. Soil is a living ecosystem of minerals, organic matter, water, air, and billions of microorganisms. Healthy soil = good crops. Dead soil = crop failure.

### Soil Testing (Low-Tech)

**Texture Test (Jar Method):**
1. Fill jar 1/3 with soil
2. Add water to 2/3 full
3. Add drop of dish soap (if available)
4. Shake vigorously
5. Let settle 24 hours
6. Layers separate:
   - Bottom: Sand (settles first, in minutes)
   - Middle: Silt (settles in hours)
   - Top: Clay (settles in days, water may stay cloudy)

**Ideal soil: ~40% sand, 40% silt, 20% clay (loam)**

**Drainage Test:**
1. Dig hole 12 inches deep
2. Fill with water
3. Time how long to drain

- Drains in less than 4 hours: Good drainage (sandy)
- Drains in 4-8 hours: Moderate drainage (loam)
- Drains in 8+ hours: Poor drainage (clay)

**pH Test (If you have salvaged test kit):**
- 6.0-7.0: Ideal for most crops
- Below 6.0: Too acidic (add wood ash or lime)
- Above 7.5: Too alkaline (add sulfur, compost)

**Without test kit:** Most crops tolerate 6.0-7.5. Focus on organic matter instead.

### Building Soil Fertility

**Organic Matter Is Everything:**

Why it matters:
- Holds moisture (reduces drought stress)
- Provides nutrients as it decomposes
- Improves drainage in clay soils
- Improves water retention in sandy soils
- Feeds soil organisms

**How to Add Organic Matter:**

1. **Compost** (see Section 5)
2. **Manure** (see Section 6)
3. **Green Manure/Cover Crops:**
   - Plant crops specifically to plow under
   - Clover, vetch: Add nitrogen
   - Rye, oats: Add organic matter, prevent erosion
   - Plant after harvest, plow under before next planting

4. **Mulch:**
   - Straw, leaves, grass clippings on soil surface
   - Prevents erosion, retains moisture, adds organic matter as it decomposes
   - 2-4 inches deep

### Crop Rotation: The Foundation of Sustainable Agriculture

**Why Rotate:**
- Different crops use different nutrients
- Breaks disease cycles (diseases are crop-specific)
- Breaks pest cycles
- Improves soil structure

**Never plant the same crop family in the same location two years in a row.**

**Basic 4-Year Rotation:**

**Year 1: Legumes (Beans, Peas)**
- Add nitrogen to soil (nitrogen-fixing bacteria in root nodules)
- Light feeders

**Year 2: Heavy Feeders (Cabbage, Corn, Tomatoes)**
- Use nitrogen left by legumes
- Add compost heavily this year

**Year 3: Root Crops (Carrots, Beets, Potatoes)**
- Break up soil with deep roots
- Moderate feeders

**Year 4: Light Feeders or Cover Crop**
- Grains, onions, or rest with cover crop
- Rebuild soil for cycle restart

**Example 4-Field System:**

Field A: Legumes → Heavy Feeders → Roots → Grains → Legumes...
Field B: Heavy Feeders → Roots → Grains → Legumes → Heavy Feeders...
Field C: Roots → Grains → Legumes → Heavy Feeders → Roots...
Field D: Grains → Legumes → Heavy Feeders → Roots → Grains...

Each field is in a different stage. Stagger your crops.

### Nutrient Management (Without Chemical Fertilizer)

**The Big Three Nutrients:**

1. **Nitrogen (N):** Leafy growth, overall vigor
   - Sources: Legume crops, manure, compost, urine (diluted)
   - Deficiency signs: Yellow leaves, stunted growth

2. **Phosphorus (P):** Root development, flowering, fruiting
   - Sources: Bone meal, manure, compost, rock phosphate
   - Deficiency signs: Purple leaves, poor fruiting

3. **Potassium (K):** Overall health, disease resistance
   - Sources: Wood ash, compost, kelp (if coastal)
   - Deficiency signs: Brown leaf edges, weak stems

**Practical Fertilizer Sources:**

**Wood Ash:**
- High in potassium and calcium
- Raises pH (don't overuse on alkaline soil)
- Application: 5-10 lbs per 100 square feet, once per year maximum
- Warning: Never use ash from treated wood (toxic)

**Manure:**
- Complete fertilizer (all nutrients)
- Must age 6-12 months (fresh manure burns plants, may contain pathogens)
- Application: 1-2 inches per year, worked into soil
- Best: Cow, horse, sheep, goat, chicken (hot, use sparingly)

**Compost:**
- See Section 5
- Complete fertilizer, soil conditioner
- Application: 1-4 inches per year

**Urine (Human/Animal):**
- High nitrogen (11-1-2.5 NPK ratio)
- Must dilute: 1 part urine to 10 parts water
- Use immediately on soil, not on edible plant parts
- Let crops grow 1 month after application before harvest
- Cultural taboo but extremely effective

**Bone Meal:**
- High phosphorus
- Salvage: Grind bones from butchered animals
- Burn bones to ash (easier to grind, releases phosphorus)
- Application: 1 cup per 10 square feet for fruiting crops

**Green Manure:**
- See cover crops above
- Plant → Grow → Till under before flowering
- Decomposes and feeds next crop

### Preventing Soil Degradation

**Erosion Control:**
- Never leave soil bare (plant or mulch)
- Contour plowing on slopes (plow across slope, not up/down)
- Terraces on steep slopes
- Cover crops in off-season

**Compaction Prevention:**
- Don't work wet soil (destroys structure)
- Minimize foot traffic
- Build raised beds or permanent paths

**Salinity (In Arid Regions):**
- Irrigation can bring salts to surface
- Ensure good drainage
- Occasional deep watering to flush salts below root zone

## 5. Composting: Turning Waste Into Black Gold

### Why Compost Matters

Compost is the single most important soil amendment you can make. It:
- Provides all nutrients plants need
- Improves soil structure
- Increases water retention
- Feeds beneficial soil organisms
- Costs nothing but labor

**A community with good composting can sustain agriculture indefinitely without outside inputs.**

### The Science (Simple Version)

- Microorganisms eat organic matter
- They breathe oxygen and give off heat
- They need water, air, carbon (browns), and nitrogen (greens)
- Result: Stable, nutrient-rich humus

### Basic Compost Recipe

**"Browns" (Carbon-Rich):**
- Dry leaves
- Straw
- Wood chips (small pieces)
- Sawdust (use sparingly, decomposes slowly)
- Shredded paper/cardboard
- Ratio: ~30 parts carbon to 1 part nitrogen (by volume, easier than it sounds)

**"Greens" (Nitrogen-Rich):**
- Fresh grass clippings
- Vegetable scraps
- Weeds (before they seed)
- Manure
- Coffee grounds (salvaged)

**Don't Compost:**
- Meat, bones, dairy (attracts pests, smells)
- Diseased plants (may survive composting)
- Perennial weeds with runners/roots
- Dog/cat feces (pathogens)
- Treated wood, plastics, metals

### Building a Compost Pile

**Location:**
- Near garden (you'll move a lot of compost)
- Partial shade (won't dry out as fast)
- Good drainage (not swampy)
- Away from buildings (can attract pests)

**Size Matters:**
- Minimum: 3 feet × 3 feet × 3 feet (27 cubic feet)
- Smaller piles won't heat properly
- Larger piles work better

**Construction Methods:**

**Method 1: Simple Pile**
1. Layer browns and greens (roughly 3:1 ratio)
2. Keep as moist as wrung-out sponge
3. Turn every 1-2 weeks (moves outside to inside)
4. Ready in 3-6 months

**Method 2: Bin System**
- Build three bins from salvaged pallets, wire, or wood
- Bin 1: Fresh materials, actively building
- Bin 2: Composting (turn into this bin from Bin 1)
- Bin 3: Finished compost, ready to use
- Cycle materials through bins

**Method 3: Trench Composting (Lazy Method)**
1. Dig trench 12 inches deep in fallow bed
2. Fill with kitchen scraps and other compostables
3. Cover with soil
4. Plant on top next season
5. Works well, no turning required, but slower

### Optimizing Compost

**Temperature Monitoring:**
- Hot compost (140-160°F): Kills weed seeds and pathogens
- Warm compost (90-140°F): Slower but still works
- Cold compost (ambient): Works but takes 6-12 months

**To heat up pile:**
- Add more greens (nitrogen)
- Add water (should be moist)
- Make pile bigger
- Turn pile (adds oxygen)

**Signs of Problems:**

**Pile smells like ammonia:**
- Too much nitrogen (greens)
- Solution: Add browns (carbon)

**Pile smells rotten:**
- Too wet, not enough air
- Solution: Turn pile, add dry browns

**Pile not heating:**
- Too small, too dry, wrong ratio
- Solution: Make bigger, add water, add greens

**Pile has flies:**
- Food scraps exposed
- Solution: Bury scraps in center, cover with browns

### Finished Compost

**Ready when:**
- Dark brown, crumbly
- Earthy smell (like forest floor)
- Original materials unrecognizable
- Cool temperature

**Using Compost:**
- Work into soil before planting: 1-4 inches
- Side-dress around growing plants: 1 inch
- Mulch on top of soil: 2-3 inches
- Potting mix: 1 part compost, 1 part soil

**Production:**
- Well-managed pile: Halves in volume
- 1 cubic yard of materials → 0.5 cubic yards compost
- Mature compost weighs ~1000 lbs per cubic yard

### Compost Tea (Liquid Fertilizer)

**Method:**
1. Fill burlap sack or cloth bag with finished compost
2. Suspend in barrel of water (5 gallons water per 1 lb compost)
3. Steep 7-14 days, stirring daily
4. Dilute to weak tea color
5. Water plants with liquid

**Benefits:**
- Quick nutrient boost
- Beneficial microorganisms
- Good for transplants

## 6. Animal Husbandry

Animals are not luxuries. They are critical components of sustainable agriculture.

**Animals provide:**
1. **Draft power:** Plow, transport, grind grain (replaces fossil fuels)
2. **Fertilizer:** High-quality manure
3. **Protein:** Meat, milk, eggs
4. **Materials:** Leather, wool, bone
5. **Tillage:** Pigs root, chickens scratch (prepare soil)

### Chickens (Start Here)

**Why chickens first:**
- Easiest to manage
- Quick return (eggs in 5-6 months)
- Eat kitchen scraps, bugs, weeds
- Excellent manure
- Small space requirement

**Starting a Flock:**

**Numbers:** Start with 6-12 hens, 1 rooster (for fertile eggs/reproduction)

**Housing:**
- 2-3 square feet per bird inside coop
- 8-10 square feet per bird in run
- Roosting bars (12 inches per bird)
- Nest boxes: 1 per 3-4 hens
- Predator-proof: Wire mesh floor, secure door

**Feeding:**
- Free-range: Chickens find ~70% of food (bugs, seeds, greens)
- Supplement: Grains (corn, wheat, oats)
- Calcium: Crushed eggshells or oyster shells (for strong egg shells)
- Grit: Small stones (chickens need these to digest)
- Water: Fresh daily

**Production:**
- Hens lay ~250 eggs per year (peak at 1-2 years old)
- 6 hens = ~35 eggs per week
- Egg production drops in winter (less daylight)

**Manure:**
- Hot (high nitrogen)
- Must compost 6-12 months before using
- Mix with bedding (straw, wood shavings)

**Breeding:**
- Hen goes "broody" (wants to sit on eggs)
- Place 10-12 fertile eggs under her
- 21 days → chicks hatch
- Or salvage incubator (maintains 99.5°F, turns eggs)

### Goats (Efficient, Versatile)

**Why goats:**
- Milk (1-2 quarts per day per doe)
- Meat
- Efficient browsers (eat brush, weeds)
- Smaller than cows (easier to manage)
- Good for rough terrain

**Starting a Herd:**

**Numbers:** 2-3 does (females), 1 buck (male, keep separate to control breeding)

**Housing:**
- Shelter from rain/wind (goats hate wet)
- 15-20 square feet per goat
- Raised platform for sleeping (keeps dry)
- Strong fencing (goats are escape artists): 4-5 feet tall, tight mesh

**Feeding:**
- Browse: Brush, weeds, leaves
- Pasture: Grass, clover
- Hay in winter
- Grain supplement for milking does
- Minerals: Salt lick, mineral block

**Milking:**
- Breed doe (goats must give birth to produce milk)
- Milk production starts after kidding (giving birth)
- Milk twice daily, same times
- Production: 1-2 quarts per day for 10 months (then re-breed)

**Breeding:**
- Does come into heat every 21 days (fall/winter in most climates)
- Gestation: 5 months
- Kids (babies): 1-3 per birth
- Wean kids at 8-12 weeks

**Manure:**
- Dry pellets (easy to handle)
- Can apply directly to soil or compost first

### Pigs (Efficient Protein, Land Clearing)

**Why pigs:**
- Convert food scraps/waste to meat (efficient)
- Rapid growth (6 months to slaughter weight)
- Till soil (rooting behavior)
- High-calorie meat, lard (cooking fat, soap-making)

**Starting:**

**Numbers:** Start with 2-3 piglets (easier to raise together)

**Housing:**
- Shelter from sun/rain
- Wallow (mud hole for cooling)
- Strong fencing (pigs are strong): 4-foot woven wire + electric top wire
- 50-100 square feet per pig

**Feeding:**
- Omnivores (eat almost anything)
- Kitchen scraps, garden waste
- Grain (corn, barley, wheat)
- Pasture (less efficient than chickens/goats but they'll eat it)
- Avoid: Meat scraps (can transmit diseases)

**Growth:**
- Piglet (weaned, ~8 weeks): 30-50 lbs
- 6 months: 250-280 lbs (slaughter weight)
- Feed conversion: ~3 lbs feed per 1 lb gain

**Breeding:**
- Keep 1-2 sows (females), 1 boar (male)
- Gestation: 3 months, 3 weeks, 3 days (~114 days)
- Litter size: 8-12 piglets
- Farrow (give birth) twice per year possible

**Manure:**
- High nitrogen
- Compost before use (can be "hot")

### Cattle (Advanced, High Resource)

**Only pursue if:**
- Large land area (1-2 acres pasture per cow)
- Established infrastructure
- Experienced animal handlers

**Benefits:**
- Milk (3-5 gallons per day, dairy breeds)
- Meat (large quantity)
- Draft power (oxen are castrated bulls)
- Leather (large hides)

**Resource requirements:**
- Pasture: 1-2 acres per animal
- Hay: 2-3 tons per animal per winter
- Strong fencing
- Shelter (simple three-sided structure)

**Draft oxen:**
- Train from young age (calves)
- 2 years to working age
- Can pull plow, wagon, logs
- Extremely valuable for large-scale agriculture

### Draft Animals: Replacing Tractors

**Why draft animals matter:**
- Tractors require fuel (not sustainable long-term)
- Draft animals reproduce themselves
- Provide multiple products (manure, meat, milk)
- Work on rough terrain

**Options:**

**Oxen (Cattle):**
- Strongest (can pull 1.5× their weight)
- Slow, steady
- Best for heavy plowing, logging

**Horses:**
- Faster than oxen
- Medium strength
- Best for cultivation, transport
- Require more food than oxen

**Donkeys/Mules:**
- Hardy, disease-resistant
- Good for rough terrain
- Mules (horse × donkey) are sterile but very strong

**Training: The Multi-Year Reality**

⚠️ **Critical: If no one in your community has draft animal experience, budget 2-3 years of trial and error, and expect to lose some animals to training accidents.**

Draft animal training is a high-skill endeavor that takes time, patience, and knowledge often transmitted orally. If you're starting from zero:

**Year 1: Finding and Gentling**
- Acquire young animals (1-2 years old ideal, calves/colts)
- If salvaging animals from old world: Feral animals are dangerous and hard to train
- Spend time daily handling (touching, leading, grooming)
- Goal: Animal is calm around humans, accepts halter/lead
- Many communities fail here (impatient, rough handling creates unusable animals)

**Year 2: Basic Training**
- **Start with yoke/harness fitting** (no load)
  - Let animal wear empty yoke/harness for short periods (30 min/day)
  - Gradually increase duration
  - Animal must accept restraint without panic
- **Voice commands** (consistent, patient repetition)
  - "Gee" = turn right
  - "Haw" = turn left
  - "Whoa" = stop
  - "Back" = reverse
  - "Get up" or "Walk on" = move forward
- **Walking training** (handler walking beside, giving commands)
- **Light dragging** (log, empty cart)
- Duration: 6-12 months daily work

**Year 3: Work Training**
- Attach to plow (empty field first)
- Short sessions (15-30 minutes) increase gradually
- Work in pairs (experienced animal + trainee if possible)
- Real plowing starts
- By year 3 end: Reliable work animal (if all goes well)

**Common Training Failures:**
1. **Rushing**: Trying to plow in Year 1 → animal panics, injures self or handler
2. **Inconsistency**: Different people giving different commands → confusion
3. **Fear/abuse**: Hitting animal → permanent fear, refusal to work
4. **Poor nutrition during training**: Weak animal can't learn, may die
5. **Working too young**: Damages growing bones, creates permanent injury

**Accident Risks:**
- Kicks (can kill handler)
- Trampling (especially large cattle)
- Equipment failure (breaking harness, animal runs off with plow)
- Panic response (animal runs through fence, injures self)

**If You Find Someone With Experience:**
- Prioritize protecting them (see Chapter -1: Knowledge Triage)
- Have them train 2-3 apprentices
- Document everything they do in writing
- This knowledge is worth its weight in gold

**Equipment:**
- Yoke (oxen) or harness (horses)
- Plow
- Cart/wagon
- All can be made from salvaged metal and wood (see Section 8)
- **Critical**: Proper fit prevents injury. Too-tight yoke causes sores, too-loose is ineffective

### Animal Health Basics

**Disease Prevention:**
- Clean housing
- Fresh water daily
- Parasite control (rotate pastures, keep dry)
- Quarantine new animals (2-4 weeks)

**Common Problems:**

**Parasites (Worms):**
- Symptoms: Weight loss, rough coat, diarrhea
- Prevention: Rotational grazing, dry bedding
- Treatment: Herbal (wormwood, tobacco—use carefully) or salvaged dewormers

**Bloat (Ruminants: Goats, Cattle):**
- Cause: Rapid fermentation in rumen (often from too much fresh legumes)
- Symptoms: Swollen left side, distress
- Prevention: Gradually introduce new feeds, provide hay with pasture
- Emergency: Drench with vegetable oil (1 cup for goat, 1 quart for cow)

**Hoof Problems:**
- Trim hooves 1-2 times per year
- Prevents lameness

**Reproduction Issues:**
- Observe heat cycles, breeding behavior
- Provide good nutrition (especially late pregnancy, lactation)
- Separate aggressive males (can injure females/young)

## 7. Irrigation Systems

Water is life. Drought kills crops. Irrigation turns marginal land productive and ensures reliable harvests.

### Assessing Water Needs

**Natural rainfall:**
- Most crops need 1 inch of water per week
- If rainfall provides this, irrigation is insurance
- If rainfall is less, irrigation is essential

**Water sources:**
1. Rivers, streams (reliable)
2. Ponds, lakes
3. Springs
4. Wells (dug or salvaged drilled)
5. Rainwater catchment

### Gravity-Fed Systems (No Pumping Required)

**Principle:** Water flows downhill. If source is higher than field, you don't need pumps.

**Method 1: Ditch Irrigation**

Construction:
1. Dig main ditch from water source along high side of field
2. Dig furrows (small channels) from main ditch into field
3. Slope furrows 1-2% (1-2 feet drop per 100 feet length)
4. Open gate in main ditch, water flows into furrows

Advantages:
- Simple (just digging)
- No mechanical parts
- Works for large areas

Disadvantages:
- Water waste (evaporation, runoff)
- Can cause erosion
- Labor intensive to dig

**Method 2: Contour Ditches (Hillside)**

Construction:
1. Mark contour lines (same elevation) across slope
2. Dig ditch along contour (level or very slight slope)
3. Water flows slowly along contour, soaks in
4. Multiple contour ditches at different elevations

Advantages:
- Controls erosion
- Waters large hillside areas
- Recharges groundwater

**Method 3: Terrace and Flood (Paddy System)**

Construction:
1. Build terraces (level steps) on hillside
2. Berm (raised edge) at downhill edge of each terrace
3. Water source feeds top terrace
4. Overflow goes to next terrace down

Advantages:
- Excellent erosion control
- Good for rice or other water-loving crops
- Maximizes hillside use

Disadvantages:
- Labor-intensive to build
- Not suitable for all crops

### Mechanical Water Lifting

When water source is below field level, you need to lift water.

**Water Wheel (River/Stream Power)**

Principle: Flowing water turns wheel, buckets lift water

Construction:
1. Build wheel 6-12 feet diameter
   - Salvaged bicycle wheels, wooden frame, etc.
2. Attach buckets around rim (cut plastic bottles, metal cans)
3. Mount wheel so bottom is in flowing water
4. Current turns wheel
5. Buckets fill at bottom, empty at top into trough
6. Trough channels water to field

Requirements:
- Stream with current
- Drop in elevation or dam to create current
- Mechanical skill to build

Output:
- Depends on wheel size, current speed
- 6-foot wheel in good current: 100-500 gallons per hour

**Hand Pump**

Construction:
- Salvage well hand pump (common in rural areas)
- Or build from pipe, leather valve, handle
- Install in well or over cistern

Labor:
- Hand-powered (labor intensive)
- Use for small gardens or filling cistern (then gravity-feed to field)

**Treadle Pump (Foot-Powered)**

Principle: Step on treadles (like bicycling while standing), pushes pistons, lifts water

Construction:
1. Two pistons in cylinders
2. Treadles connected to pistons
3. Valves in cylinders (open up, close down)
4. Intake hose to water source
5. Discharge hose to field

Advantages:
- More efficient than hand pump (legs stronger than arms)
- Can pump 2-7 meters lift
- Flow: 1000-2000 liters per hour (1-2 people)

Materials:
- Salvaged PVC pipes (cylinders)
- Leather or rubber (valves)
- Wood or metal (frame, treadles)

**Windmill (If Wind Available)**

Construction:
- Salvage: Old windmills still exist
- Or build: Fan blades on vertical shaft, connected to pump
- Store water in elevated tank, gravity-feed to field

Advantages:
- No labor once built
- Works day and night (if wind blows)

Disadvantages:
- Complex construction
- Requires steady wind
- Maintenance

**Animal-Powered (Advanced)**

Persian wheel (saqiya):
- Animal walks in circle
- Turns horizontal wheel (geared to vertical wheel)
- Vertical wheel has buckets
- Lifts water continuously

Advantages:
- High volume
- No human labor during operation

Disadvantages:
- Requires trained draft animal
- Complex gearing

### Distribution Methods

**Furrow Irrigation:**
- Dig furrows between crop rows
- Run water down furrows
- Simple, wasteful, can cause erosion

**Flood Irrigation:**
- Flood entire field
- Only for level fields, water-tolerant crops
- Very wasteful

**Drip Irrigation (Low-Tech Version):**
1. Bury clay pots (unglazed) near plants
2. Fill pots with water
3. Water seeps through clay slowly
4. Very efficient (almost no waste)

Alternative:
- Poke small holes in salvaged plastic bottles
- Bury near plants
- Fill with water
- Slow release

**Mulch (Water Conservation):**
- 2-4 inches straw, leaves, grass clippings around plants
- Reduces evaporation by 50-70%
- Essentially "free" water savings

### Rainwater Catchment

**Calculation:**
- 1 inch rain on 1000 sq ft roof = 600 gallons water
- Average roof: 1500-2000 sq ft
- One rain: 900-1200 gallons

**System:**

1. **Gutters:** Channel water from roof (salvaged or make from sheet metal)
2. **Downspouts:** Direct to storage
3. **First-Flush Diverter:** Diverts first gallon (washes roof dirt away)
4. **Storage:** Tanks, barrels, cisterns (55-gallon drums, IBC totes, underground cisterns)
5. **Distribution:** Gravity-feed to garden

**Storage calculation:**
- 10-person community needs ~30 gallons/person/day = 300 gallons/day
- Irrigation needs: Varies widely (500-2000 gallons/day for 0.5 acre garden in dry season)
- Storage goal: At least 2-week supply without rain

## 8. Tool Making for Agriculture

Modern tools will break. You must make and repair agricultural tools.

### Essential Agricultural Tools

**Priority order:**
1. Hoe (weeding, cultivation)
2. Spade/shovel (digging)
3. Rake (soil preparation, harvesting)
4. Scythe (cutting grain, grass)
5. Sickle (cutting small grains, harvesting)
6. Plow (turning soil, large-scale)
7. Harrow (breaking clods, smoothing)
8. Flail (threshing grain)

### Making Tools from Salvage

**Materials needed:**
- Steel (salvaged leaf springs from vehicles—excellent tool steel)
- Iron rods/pipe (handles, structural)
- Wood (handles)
- Fasteners (bolts, rivets, welds)

**Workshop requirements:**
- Forge (see Chapter 2: Metallurgy)
- Anvil
- Hammer
- Files
- Quenching bucket

### Hoe Construction

**Blade:**
1. Source: Leaf spring from car/truck (high-carbon steel)
2. Heat in forge to red heat
3. Flatten to 1/8 inch thick
4. Shape to 6-8 inches wide, 6 inches deep
5. Sharpen leading edge (file or grind)
6. Heat to critical temperature (non-magnetic)
7. Quench in oil (hardens)
8. Temper: Reheat to straw color, quench (toughens, less brittle)

**Handle:**
1. Straight wood handle 4-5 feet long
2. Hardwood (oak, hickory, ash) best but any solid wood works
3. Carve socket in blade or drill hole
4. Insert handle, secure with wedge or rivet

**Assembly:**
- Blade angle: 70-80 degrees to handle
- Secure tightly (blade will take shock)

### Scythe (Critical for Grain Harvest)

**Blade:**
- 24-36 inches long, curved
- Thin (1/16 inch or less)
- Very sharp
- Salvage: Lawn mower blades (reshape), leaf springs (flatten and curve)

**Snath (Handle):**
- 5-6 feet long, curved or angled
- Two hand grips (adjust for user height)
- Wood (traditionally willow or ash)

**Sharpening:**
- Peening: Hammer edge thin on small anvil (mushrooms edge)
- Honing: Whetstone, stroke along edge
- Sharpen every 5-10 minutes while cutting (critical!)

**Use:**
- Stand upright, swing blade in arc parallel to ground
- Blade floats just above ground, cuts stems
- Practiced user: 1 acre per day

### Plow (For Draft Animals or Hand Use)

**Types:**

**Moldboard Plow (Turns Soil):**
- Blade cuts furrow
- Moldboard turns soil over
- Buries weeds, organic matter

**Ard/Scratch Plow (Simple):**
- Pointed blade scratches groove
- Doesn't turn soil (less effective but easier to make)

**Construction (Simple Scratch Plow):**

1. **Point:** Steel, sharpened (car/truck A-frame ideal)
2. **Beam:** Wooden pole, 6-8 feet (connects to animal/person)
3. **Handle:** Vertical pole for steering
4. **Assembly:** Point attaches to front of beam, penetrates soil

**Construction (Moldboard Plow - Advanced):**

1. **Share (blade):** Cuts horizontal into soil
   - Angled steel, sharpened edge
   - Salvage: Heavy plate steel, cut and shape

2. **Moldboard:** Curved surface, turns soil over
   - Salvage: Sheet metal, curved
   - Or carve from hardwood

3. **Beam:** Wooden frame connecting all parts
4. **Handles:** Two handles for steering
5. **Hitch:** Connection to draft animal

**Adjustment:**
- Depth control: Adjust point angle
- Width of furrow: Adjust share width

**Use:**
- Draft animal pulls plow
- Operator steers, maintains depth
- Cuts furrow ~6-8 inches deep
- 1-2 acres per day with animal power

### Harrow (Breaking Clods)

**Spike Harrow:**
- Frame (wood or metal)
- Spikes pointing down (nails, rebar, sharpened wood)
- Drag across plowed field
- Breaks clods, smooths surface

**Construction:**
1. Build rectangular frame 4 feet × 4 feet
2. Drill holes every 6 inches
3. Insert spikes (12-18 inches long)
4. Hitch to drag

### Threshing Tools

**Flail:**
- Handle: 4-5 feet long
- Striker: 2-3 feet long, heavy wood
- Connected by leather strip or short chain
- Use: Beat grain heads on hard surface, separates grain from straw

**Threshing Floor:**
- Hard, clean surface
- Spread grain sheaves
- Beat with flail or have animals walk on it
- Straw separates from grain

**Winnowing:**
- Toss threshed grain in air
- Wind blows away chaff (lightweight)
- Grain (heavy) falls back down
- Collect clean grain

## 9. Pest Control Without Chemicals

Chemical pesticides are not available or sustainable. Integrated pest management is essential.

### Prevention First

**Strategy 1: Healthy Plants**
- Healthy plants resist pests better
- Good soil, proper watering, adequate spacing

**Strategy 2: Diversity**
- Monocultures attract pests
- Mix crops (confuses pests, provides predator habitat)
- Rotate crops (breaks pest cycles)

**Strategy 3: Timing**
- Plant when pests less active
- Early or late planting can avoid peak pest populations

### Physical Barriers

**Row Covers:**
- Lightweight fabric over crops
- Salvaged: Curtains, sheets, shade cloth
- Prevents flying insects from reaching plants
- Remove when plants flower (need pollination)

**Collars:**
- Paper/cardboard collar around seedling stems
- Prevents cutworms from severing stems

**Netting:**
- Over fruiting plants
- Prevents bird damage

**Hand Picking:**
- Labor-intensive but effective
- Check plants daily
- Remove beetles, caterpillars, egg masses
- Drop in soapy water to kill

### Biological Controls

**Encourage Predators:**

**Birds:**
- Nest boxes attract insect-eating birds
- Perches in garden
- Note: Need netting over fruiting crops

**Beneficial Insects:**
- Ladybugs: Eat aphids
- Lacewings: Eat aphids, mites
- Ground beetles: Eat cutworms, slugs
- Provide habitat: Flowers, undisturbed areas

**Chickens/Ducks:**
- Free-range in garden (before planting or after harvest)
- Eat bugs, larvae, slugs
- Fertilize while they work
- Note: Will eat seedlings, so timing matters

### Companion Planting

**Marigolds:**
- Plant throughout garden
- Repel many insects (scent)
- Attract beneficial predators

**Alliums (Onions, Garlic):**
- Repel aphids, beetles
- Interplant with susceptible crops

**Nasturtiums:**
- Trap crop for aphids
- Plant away from main crop
- Aphids prefer nasturtiums, leave vegetables alone

**Three Sisters (Indigenous Agricultural Knowledge):**

This is documented Indigenous agricultural knowledge (primarily Haudenosaunee/Iroquois and other Native American peoples) that sustained large populations for millennia. Acknowledging the source honors the tradition and reinforces credibility.

**How it works:**
- **Corn** grows tall, provides structure for beans to climb
- **Beans** climb corn stalks, fix nitrogen from air (improves soil for next year)
- **Squash** spreads along ground, large leaves shade soil (prevents weeds, retains moisture)
- **Together**: Mutually beneficial, higher yield than monoculture, sustainable long-term

**Planting method:**
1. Plant corn first (when soil is warm, after last frost)
2. When corn is 6 inches tall, plant beans around base (4-6 beans per corn stalk)
3. One week after beans, plant squash between corn hills
4. Space: Corn hills 3-4 feet apart

**Benefits:**
- Nutritional completeness (corn = carbs, beans = protein, squash = vitamins)
- Soil improvement (beans add nitrogen)
- Pest reduction (squash leaves deter raccoons, diversity confuses pests)
- Water efficiency (squash reduces evaporation)

**This system worked for centuries before European contact. Learn from it.**

### Homemade Sprays and Treatments

**Soap Spray (Aphids, Mites):**
1. 1-2 tablespoons pure soap (not detergent) per quart water
2. Spray on affected plants
3. Coats insects, suffocates
4. Rinse off after 2-3 hours (can damage leaves if left)

**Garlic Spray (General Repellent):**
1. Blend 1 bulb garlic with 2 cups water
2. Strain
3. Add 1 quart water
4. Add drop of soap (helps stick)
5. Spray on plants
6. Reapply after rain

**Hot Pepper Spray (Mammals, Some Insects):**
1. Blend hot peppers with water
2. Strain
3. Add drop of soap
4. Spray on plants
5. **Warning:** Wash hands, avoid eyes, wear gloves

**Diatomaceous Earth (Slugs, Beetles):**
- Fossilized algae, sharp microscopic edges
- Salvage from swimming pool supply stores
- Dust on plants and soil
- Cuts soft-bodied pests
- Reapply after rain

**Wood Ash (Slugs, Some Beetles):**
- Ring around plants
- Slugs won't cross (too dry, alkaline)
- Also adds potassium to soil

### Specific Pest Solutions

**Aphids:**
- Spray off with water
- Soap spray
- Ladybugs (predator)
- Companion plant: Nasturtiums

**Cutworms:**
- Collars around seedlings
- Hand pick at night (active then)
- Till soil before planting (exposes to birds)

**Slugs/Snails:**
- Hand pick at night
- Beer traps (shallow dish of beer, they drown)
- Copper strips (they won't cross)
- Diatomaceous earth
- Ducks (eat slugs voraciously)

**Caterpillars:**
- Hand pick
- Row covers
- Encourage birds

**Beetles:**
- Hand pick into soapy water
- Trap crops
- Chickens

**Rabbits/Rodents:**
- Fence (buried 6 inches underground, 3 feet high)
- Traps
- Dogs or cats

**Deer:**
- Tall fence (8 feet minimum)
- Multiple shorter fences (deer won't jump if unsure of landing)
- Human hair hung in bags (scent deterrent)
- Hot pepper spray on plants

**Crows/Birds:**
- Netting over crops
- Scarecrows (move frequently, or birds learn)
- Predator decoys (owl statue)

### Disease Management

**Fungal Diseases (Powdery Mildew, Blight):**

Prevention:
- Space plants (air circulation)
- Water soil, not leaves
- Remove infected leaves immediately
- Rotate crops

Treatment:
- Baking soda spray: 1 tablespoon baking soda + 1 drop soap per quart water
- Spray weekly

**Bacterial Diseases:**
- Hard to treat
- Prevention: Crop rotation, remove infected plants
- Don't work wet plants (spreads disease)

**Viral Diseases:**
- No treatment
- Remove infected plants immediately
- Control aphids (transmit viruses)

## 10. Food Storage at Scale

Personal survival storage (Chapter -1) is not enough. You need to store surplus to feed community through winter and bad years.

### Grain Storage

Grain is civilization's foundation. Proper storage prevents famine.

**Enemies of Stored Grain:**
1. Moisture (mold, sprouting)
2. Insects (weevils, moths)
3. Rodents (mice, rats)
4. Heat (accelerates spoilage)

**Moisture Control:**

**Drying grain:**
- Grain must be dry before storage (13% moisture or less)
- Test: Bite kernel. Should crack, not dent.
- If not dry enough, spread in sun on clean surface
- Turn daily
- Takes 3-7 days depending on weather

**Storage containers:**
- Metal drums (ideal—salvage 55-gallon drums)
- Large ceramic crocks with lids
- Wooden bins (must be very tight-fitting lid)
- Avoid: Plastic bags (rodents chew through)

**Container preparation:**
1. Clean thoroughly
2. Ensure completely dry
3. Check for holes/cracks

**Insect Control:**

**Method 1: Freezing (if possible)**
- Freeze grain at 0°F for 3 days
- Kills all life stages
- Then store normally

**Method 2: Dry Ice (CO2)**
- Available from salvage (fire extinguishers contain CO2)
- Place 1 lb dry ice per 5 cubic feet grain in bottom of container
- Pour grain on top
- Leave lid loose until dry ice sublimates (becomes gas)
- CO2 heavier than air, displaces oxygen
- Insects suffocate
- Seal lid

**Method 3: Diatomaceous Earth**
- Mix 1 cup diatomaceous earth per 40 lbs grain
- Kills insects mechanically (sharp edges cut them)
- Food-grade only
- Safe to eat after washing grain

**Method 4: Heat**
- Heat grain to 120-140°F for 30 minutes
- Kills insects and eggs
- Don't overheat (reduces germination)

**Rodent Control:**

**Prevention:**
1. Metal containers (rodents can't chew)
2. Elevated storage (12+ inches off ground)
3. Keep area clean (no spilled grain)
4. Remove nesting materials near storage

**Active control:**
- Traps (snap traps, bucket traps)
- Cats (highly effective)
- Seal all entry points to storage building

**Storage Location:**

Ideal:
- Cool (below 60°F extends storage life)
- Dry (low humidity)
- Dark
- Ventilated (prevents moisture buildup)
- Secure from pests and theft

**Storage Life (Properly Stored):**
- Wheat: 20+ years
- Corn: 10+ years
- Rice: 20+ years
- Beans: 5-10 years (longer if frozen periodically)
- Oats: 8-10 years

**Monitoring:**
- Check monthly
- Look for: Moisture, insects, rodent damage, off smells
- Rotate stock (first in, first out)

### Root Cellar Storage (Review from Chapter -1)

**Construction review:**
- Underground or partially underground
- Temperature: 32-40°F
- Humidity: 85-95%
- Ventilation: Essential

**Crops stored:**
- Potatoes: 6-8 months
- Carrots, beets (in sand): 4-6 months
- Cabbage: 3-4 months
- Apples (some varieties): 3-6 months
- Winter squash (cured): 3-6 months

**Storage tips:**
- Don't wash (soil protects)
- Check weekly, remove any rotting
- Keep varieties separate
- Maintain humidity (mist floor if needed)

### Drying (Expanded)

**Large-Scale Solar Dehydrator:**

Construction:
1. Box 4 feet × 8 feet × 1 foot deep
2. Bottom: Dark metal (absorbs heat)
3. Sides: Wood
4. Top: Clear plastic or glass (angled for drainage)
5. Vents: Bottom (cool air in) and top (hot moist air out)
6. Trays: Screen or cloth racks inside

Operation:
- Sun heats dark bottom
- Air flows in bottom vent, heats, rises
- Hot air dries food on trays
- Moist air exits top vent
- Faster and more reliable than open-air drying

**Foods to dry:**
- Fruits: Apples, berries, plums
- Vegetables: Tomatoes, peppers, onions
- Herbs: All varieties
- Meat: Jerky (must be very dry)

**Testing dryness:**
- Fruits: Leathery, pliable, no moisture when squeezed
- Vegetables: Brittle
- Herbs: Crumble easily

**Storage:**
- Airtight containers
- Cool, dark, dry
- Check monthly for moisture
- Lasts 6-12 months

### Smoking (Expanded for Scale)

**Large Smokehouse:**

Design:
- Building 6 feet × 8 feet × 8 feet tall
- Racks throughout for hanging meat
- Fire pit outside (cold smoke) or inside bottom (hot smoke)
- Vent at top (controllable)
- Tight-fitting door

**Cold Smoking (Below 90°F):**
- Preserves, doesn't cook
- Takes days to weeks
- Excellent for long-term storage
- Must salt heavily first

**Hot Smoking (180-220°F):**
- Cooks and preserves
- Takes hours to days
- Tastier, shorter storage life

**Capacity:**
- Large smokehouse: 500+ lbs meat
- Can process entire large animal

## 11. Fermentation for Preservation

Fermentation is controlled rot. You're cultivating good bacteria that preserve food and prevent bad bacteria from growing.

### Principles of Fermentation

**Requirements:**
1. Anaerobic environment (no oxygen)
2. Salt (inhibits bad bacteria, allows good bacteria)
3. Time (bacteria need days to weeks)

**Safety:**
- If it smells rotten (not tangy/sour), discard
- If it has mold, discard (unless expected, like on salami)
- When in doubt, throw it out

### Sauerkraut (Detailed)

**Ingredients:**
- Cabbage
- Salt (2-3% by weight: 3 tablespoons per 5 lbs cabbage)

**Equipment:**
- Crock, jar, or barrel
- Weight to keep cabbage submerged
- Cloth cover

**Procedure:**
1. Remove outer leaves of cabbage
2. Shred finely (knife or mandoline)
3. Weigh cabbage
4. Calculate salt (2.5%: 5 lbs cabbage = 2 oz / ~3.5 tablespoons salt)
5. Mix cabbage and salt in bowl
6. Massage vigorously 5-10 minutes (breaks cells, releases liquid)
7. Pack tightly into crock (no air pockets)
8. Liquid should cover cabbage (add 2% brine if needed: 1 tablespoon salt per 2 cups water)
9. Weight down (plate with rock, water-filled jar, etc.)
10. Cover with cloth (keeps flies out, allows gas out)
11. Keep at room temperature (65-75°F ideal)
12. Check daily: Remove any scum, ensure cabbage submerged
13. Taste after 1 week (should be tangy)
14. Ferment 1-4 weeks total (depends on temperature, preference)
15. Move to cool storage (below 60°F) or can in jars

**Troubleshooting:**

**Mold on surface:**
- Skim off
- Check salt level (may be too low)
- Ensure cabbage submerged

**Slimy texture:**
- Too warm
- Restart with fresh cabbage, cooler location

**Too salty:**
- Rinse before eating
- Use less salt next batch (but not below 2%)

### Kimchi (Variation)

Same process as sauerkraut, plus:
- Garlic
- Ginger
- Hot pepper (if available)
- Other vegetables (radish, carrots, onions)

### Pickles (Lacto-Fermented, Not Vinegar)

**Ingredients:**
- Cucumbers (or other vegetables)
- Brine: 3-5% salt (2-3.5 tablespoons per quart water)
- Optional: Dill, garlic, grape leaves (tannins keep pickles crisp)

**Procedure:**
1. Make brine (dissolve salt in water)
2. Pack cucumbers in jar
3. Add flavorings
4. Pour brine over (ensure cucumbers submerged)
5. Weight down
6. Cover with cloth
7. Ferment 1-2 weeks at room temperature
8. Move to cool storage

### Sourdough Starter (Foundation of Bread Without Commercial Yeast)

**Creating starter:**
1. Mix equal parts flour and water (1 cup each)
2. Leave in warm location, covered with cloth
3. Daily: Discard half, add fresh flour and water
4. After 5-7 days: Bubbling, sour smell = ready

**Maintaining:**
- Feed weekly if room temperature
- Feed monthly if refrigerated
- Use for bread, pancakes, etc.

**Bread making:**
1. Night before: Mix starter, flour, water, salt
2. Let rise overnight
3. Morning: Shape, let rise 1-2 hours
4. Bake

**Why it matters:**
- No commercial yeast needed
- Starter lasts indefinitely
- Better nutrition (fermentation breaks down phytic acid)

### Other Fermented Foods

**Yogurt (If You Have Milk):**
1. Heat milk to 180°F (kills bad bacteria)
2. Cool to 110°F
3. Add yogurt culture (previous batch or salvaged yogurt)
4. Keep warm (100-110°F) 4-8 hours
5. Refrigerate

**Cheese (Simple Farmer's Cheese):**
1. Heat milk to 175-180°F
2. Add acid (vinegar or lemon juice)
3. Curds form
4. Strain through cloth
5. Press to remove liquid
6. Salt and eat fresh or age

**Beer/Alcohol (Advanced - Governance Required):**

Alcohol has **legitimate technical and medical uses** but also serious social risks. Establish governance around production and distribution BEFORE beginning.

**Legitimate Uses:**
- **Antiseptic/disinfectant**: 60-70% alcohol kills bacteria (wound cleaning, tool sterilization)
- **Solvent**: Herbal medicine extraction (tinctures), cleaning agents
- **Fuel**: Can run some engines (ethanol)
- **Trade goods**: Valuable for barter with other communities
- **Morale**: Moderate use in celebration (social cohesion)

**Social Risks:**
- **Alcoholism**: Can destroy individuals and communities
- **Resource diversion**: Takes grain that could feed people
- **Violence**: Intoxication leads to accidents, fights, poor decisions
- **Production secrets**: Can lead to dangerous homemade stills (explosions, methanol poisoning)

**Governance Framework (Establish FIRST):**
1. **Community decision**: Does community vote to allow production? (Not individual choice)
2. **Production control**: Who can produce? (Licensed/trained only)
3. **Distribution limits**: Ration system? (X amount per person per month)
4. **Medical/technical reserve**: Dedicated supply for antiseptic/solvent use (not drinking)
5. **Consequences**: What happens for abuse? (Clear, enforced rules)
6. **Review period**: Reassess every 6-12 months (is it helping or hurting?)

**Basic Beer Production (If Approved):**
1. **Malting**: Sprout grain (barley), dry, roast
2. **Mashing**: Mix malted grain with hot water (converts starch to sugar)
3. **Boiling**: Boil liquid, add hops (bittering, preservation)
4. **Fermentation**: Cool, add yeast, ferment 1-2 weeks
5. **Bottling**: Transfer to bottles, age 2+ weeks

**Safety Warnings:**
- **Never distill without expertise**: Methanol (first fraction) is poisonous, causes blindness/death
- **Pressure hazards**: Fermentation produces CO₂, sealed containers can explode
- **Fire hazard**: High-proof alcohol is flammable

**Recommendation**: Delay alcohol production until Year 5+ when food security is stable and social structures are mature. Premature alcohol production has destroyed communities.

## 13. Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Agricultural mistakes fall into two categories: **immediate catastrophic failures** (cause collapse in Year 1-2) and **gradual insidious decline** (cause collapse in Year 3-10). Both are deadly, but the gradual ones are harder to recognize until it's too late.

### Part A: Immediate Catastrophic Failures (Year 1-2)

These mistakes cause rapid, visible collapse. Communities usually recognize them, but often too late to fix.

**Mistake 1: Monoculture**

**Error:** Plant only one crop (all potatoes, all corn)

**Result:** Single pest or disease wipes out all food in days/weeks

**Solution:** Diversity. Multiple crops, multiple varieties, different locations.

**Warning sign**: "We planted 3 acres of potatoes. Late blight hit. All dead in 2 weeks. Nothing to eat."

**Mistake 2: Inadequate Storage**

**Error:** Good harvest, but lose 50% to rot, pests, moisture

**Result:** Starve despite good production

**Solution:** Build proper storage BEFORE harvest. Root cellars, grain bins, preservation techniques must be ready when crops mature.

**Warning sign**: "We harvested 500 pounds of potatoes. Stored in pile in shed. Mice ate half, rest rotted. We starve in January."

**Mistake 3: No Surplus Buffer**

**Error:** Produce exactly enough, one bad year = famine

**Result:** First drought/frost/pest outbreak causes starvation

**Solution:** Always aim for 2× needs minimum. Store surplus. Bad years WILL come.

**Warning sign**: "We produced enough for this year. Next year had late frost. Everything died. We have no reserve."

**Mistake 4: Poor Water Management**

**Error:** Rely entirely on rainfall in marginal climate

**Result:** Drought year = total crop failure

**Solution:** Irrigation system even if rainfall usually sufficient. Drought insurance.

**Warning sign**: "It always rains in June. This year it didn't. Everything died."

**Mistake 5: Tool Failure at Critical Time**

**Error:** Break critical tool (scythe, plow) at harvest, can't repair/replace

**Result:** Lose harvest window, crops rot in field

**Solution:** Make backups, learn tool repair/making, prioritize metallurgy

**Warning sign**: "Our scythe broke during wheat harvest. By the time we fixed it, grain had fallen and sprouted. Total loss."

### Part B: Gradual Insidious Decline (Year 3-10)

These mistakes don't cause immediate failure. They cause slow degradation that becomes catastrophic years later. **These are harder to recognize because early years seem fine.**

**Mistake 6: Neglecting Soil Health**

**Error:** Mine soil of nutrients, no replenishment

**Result:** Yields crash slowly Year 2-3-4, eventual famine

**How it happens:**
- Year 1: Great harvest (mining stored soil nutrients)
- Year 2: Good harvest (nutrients depleting)
- Year 3: Okay harvest (soil degrading)
- Year 4: Poor harvest (soil exhausted)
- Year 5: Famine (soil dead)

**Solution:** Compost, manure, crop rotation from Day 1. Not optional.

**Warning sign**: "Our yields were great Year 1. They're getting worse each year. We don't know why." (Answer: Soil death)

**Mistake 7: Poor Seed Saving (Genetic Drift)**

**Error:** Don't select seeds carefully, save from weakest plants, cross-contamination

**Result:** Crops get progressively worse each generation, eventual failure

**How it happens:**
- Year 1: Plant good salvaged seeds, good harvest
- Year 2: Save seeds carelessly (from first plants harvested, not best plants), okay harvest
- Year 3: Seeds from Year 2 are inferior, mediocre harvest
- Year 4: Seeds from Year 3 are worse, poor harvest
- Year 5: Seeds from Year 4 barely grow, crop failure

**Solution:** Save seed ONLY from best plants of best varieties. Store properly. Test viability yearly.

**Warning sign**: "Our corn used to be 7 feet tall. Now it's 4 feet. Ears are tiny. What happened?" (Answer: Genetic degradation)

**Mistake 8: Neglecting Animals (Loss of Draft Power)**

**Error:** Focus only on crops, skip animal husbandry

**Result:** Can't expand beyond hand labor, soil degrades (no manure), nutritional deficiency (no protein diversity)

**How it happens:**
- Year 1-2: Hand labor works for small plot
- Year 3: Want to expand, but hand labor can't scale
- Year 4: Soil degrading without manure, yields dropping
- Year 5: Malnutrition from grain-only diet, community weakening
- Year 6: Can't compete with communities that have draft animals

**Solution:** Start chickens Year 1, goats Year 2, draft animals Year 3-4. Not luxuries, necessities.

**Warning sign**: "We can only farm 2 acres because that's all we can dig by hand. Other community has oxen, they farm 20 acres with same labor."

**Mistake 9: Ignoring Crop Rotation (Soil Disease Buildup)**

**Error:** Plant same crops in same location every year

**Result:** Soil-borne diseases accumulate, yields crash

**How it happens:**
- Year 1: Plant potatoes in Field A, good yield
- Year 2: Plant potatoes in Field A again, okay yield (disease starting)
- Year 3: Plant potatoes in Field A again, poor yield (disease established)
- Year 4: Plant potatoes in Field A again, crop failure (disease saturation)

**Solution:** 4-year rotation minimum (see Section 4). Never plant same family consecutively.

**Warning sign**: "Field A used to produce great. Same crop, same care, but now it fails. Must be bad luck." (Answer: Soil disease from no rotation)

## 14. Safety Considerations

### Physical Injuries

**Draft animals:**
- Can kick, bite, crush
- Never stand directly behind animal
- Wear sturdy boots (protects from hooves)
- Children must be supervised around large animals

**Hand tools:**
- Scythes, sickles, axes are extremely sharp
- Work away from others
- Proper training essential
- First aid kit ready

**Heavy lifting:**
- Grain sacks (60-100 lbs) can injure back
- Proper technique (lift with legs, not back)
- Two people for heavy loads

### Food Safety

**Fermentation:**
- Mold (except on some foods like salami) means failure
- Throw out if in doubt
- Proper salt ratios critical

**Canning (If Using Heat Canning):**
- Low-acid foods (meat, vegetables) require pressure canning (240°F+)
- Botulism risk if undercooled
- High-acid foods (fruits, pickles, tomatoes) can water-bath can

**Grain mold:**
- Some molds produce aflatoxins (carcinogenic, deadly)
- Never eat moldy grain
- Prevent with proper drying and storage

### Chemical Hazards

**Lye (for soap-making):**
- Caustic (burns skin, blinds if splashed in eyes)
- Wear eye protection
- Work outdoors or in ventilated area
- Have vinegar ready (neutralizes lye)

**Manure:**
- Fresh manure can contain pathogens
- Always age 6-12 months
- Wash hands after handling
- Don't apply to crops within 120 days of harvest

**Pesticide alternatives:**
- Hot pepper spray: Avoid eyes, wash hands
- Diatomaceous earth: Dust mask (lung irritant if inhaled)

## 16. Closing Thoughts

Agriculture is not glamorous. It's hard physical labor, day after day, season after season. But it is the absolute foundation of everything that follows in this guide.

**You cannot rush this.** Year 1 will be hard. Year 2 will be slightly better. Year 5, you'll start to see real progress. Year 10, you'll have a stable system.

**The goal is not to farm forever.** The goal is to farm efficiently enough that most people don't have to farm. That frees them to salvage electronics, build forges, experiment with vacuum tubes, and eventually rebuild computers.

**Every hour spent improving agriculture is an hour invested in future technology.** Better soil = higher yields = more surplus = more specialists = faster progress.

Be patient. Document everything. Teach the next generation. Save your seeds.

When you can reliably feed your community with 30% of your labor force, you're ready to seriously pursue the technologies in the following chapters.

Until then, focus on this chapter. Agriculture is Tier 0 for a reason. It comes before everything else.
