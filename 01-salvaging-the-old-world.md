# Chapter 1: Salvaging the Old World

## Why Salvage Matters

The collapsed civilization of 2025 left behind an incredible treasure: billions of tons of refined materials. Mining copper ore and smelting it takes enormous energy and infrastructure. A single abandoned car contains 20-30 kg of steel, 10-15 kg of iron, 10-20 kg of aluminum, 1-2 kg of copper, and smaller amounts of zinc, lead, and precious metals.

**Core principle**: Never mine what you can salvage. The energy cost of extraction and refinement has already been paid.

## Safety First: Hazards in the Ruins

Before you begin salvaging, understand the dangers:

### Structural Hazards
- **Collapsed buildings**: Can collapse further. Never enter alone. Use support beams.
- **Unstable floors**: Rot and corrosion weaken structures. Test every step.
- **Falling debris**: Wear hard hats. Be aware of overhead hazards.

### Chemical Hazards
- **Asbestos**: Common in pre-2000 buildings (insulation, floor tiles, roofing). Causes lung cancer. Wear respirators. Wet it before disturbing.
- **Lead paint**: In older buildings. Wear gloves. Don't burn it.
- **Refrigerants**: CFCs and other coolants in refrigerators and air conditioners. Toxic when heated.
- **Batteries**: Lead-acid, lithium, and alkaline batteries contain corrosive and toxic materials. Handle carefully.
- **Unknown chemicals**: Industrial sites may have containers of acids, bases, solvents, or poisons. If you can't identify it, leave it.

### Electrical Hazards
- **Capacitors**: Large capacitors in electronics can hold lethal charges for years. Discharge before handling.
- **Batteries**: Even old batteries can cause shocks or shorts. Insulate terminals.

### Biological Hazards
- **Mold and fungus**: Wear masks in enclosed spaces.
- **Animal nests**: Rodents and insects carry disease. Be cautious.
- **Decomposition**: Avoid areas with remains. Disease risk.

### Radiation Hazards
- **Smoke detectors**: Contain americium-241. Small amounts but avoid breaking them.
- **Old gauges**: Some industrial and aircraft instruments use radium or tritium. If it glows in the dark, be very careful.
- **Medical equipment**: X-ray machines and some medical devices contain radioactive materials. Mark and avoid.
- **Industrial sources**: Nuclear density gauges, thickness sensors. Leave these alone unless you have radiation detection equipment.

**Always**: Work in teams. Establish emergency signals. Have first aid ready. Document hazardous locations and warn others.

## Priority Materials for Salvage

### Tier 1: Critical Materials (Salvage First)

**Copper Wire and Cable**
- Location: Electrical wiring in buildings, power distribution cables, electric motors, transformers
- Why critical: Excellent conductor, essential for all electrical work, cannot be easily substituted
- How to extract: Strip insulation (burn in well-ventilated area or use mechanical strippers). Sort by gauge.
- Quantity needed: Hundreds of kilograms for a small community
- Note: Copper oxidizes to green patina but remains usable. Can be cleaned with acid.

**Iron and Steel**
- Location: Vehicles, building frames, rebar, tools, appliances, filing cabinets, shelving
- Why critical: Structural material, tools, machine components
- Types to collect:
  - Mild steel (most common): Easy to work, magnetic
  - Stainless steel (sinks, cookware): Corrosion-resistant, harder to work
  - Tool steel (cutting tools, dies): Can be resharpened and reused
  - Cast iron (engine blocks, pipes): Brittle but machinable
- Quality check: Look for rust. Surface rust is fine, but deeply corroded pieces may be structurally weak.

**Aluminum**
- Location: Window frames, siding, car body panels, aircraft parts, pots and pans
- Why critical: Lightweight, corrosion-resistant, good conductor, low melting point makes it easy to recast
- Note: Different alloys for different purposes. Aircraft aluminum is highest quality.

**Glass**
- Location: Windows, bottles, laboratory glassware, optical lenses
- Why critical: Essential for chemistry, optics, vacuum tubes, displays
- Types:
  - Window glass (soda-lime): Most common
  - Laboratory glassware (borosilicate/Pyrex): Heat-resistant
  - Lenses and optics: High-quality glass with precise shapes
  - Fiber optic cable: Contains ultra-pure glass
- Handling: Wrap carefully to prevent breakage. Sort by type and quality.

**Magnets**
- Location: Loudspeakers, electric motors, hard drives, magnetic latches, refrigerator magnets
- Why critical: Essential for generators, motors, and magnetic data storage. Difficult to manufacture.
- Types:
  - Neodymium (hard drives, modern electronics): Extremely strong
  - Ferrite (ceramic magnets in speakers): Weaker but adequate
  - Alnico (older equipment): Good temperature stability
- Extraction: Hard drives contain multiple small neodymium magnets. Speakers have large ferrite magnets.

### Tier 2: Important Materials

**Precious Metals**
- Gold: Circuit boards (edge connectors, pins), cell phones, computers. Excellent conductor, never corrodes.
- Silver: Older electronics, switches, contacts. Best conductor of all metals.
- Platinum/Palladium: Catalytic converters in vehicles. Needed for certain chemical processes.
- Extraction: Complex chemical process (covered in Chapter 4). Collect electronic scrap first, extract later.

**Plastics and Polymers**
- Location: Everywhere in modern world
- Types:
  - PET (bottles): Marked with recycling symbol 1. Clear, tough.
  - HDPE (milk jugs, pipes): Symbol 2. Flexible, chemical-resistant.
  - PVC (pipes, vinyl): Symbol 3. Rigid, long-lasting. Contains chlorine.
  - LDPE (bags, squeeze bottles): Symbol 4. Very flexible.
  - PP (containers, car parts): Symbol 5. Heat-resistant.
  - PS (foam, disposable cups): Symbol 6. Insulating.
- Uses: Insulation, containers, seals, chemical resistance
- Note: Can be melted and reformed. Keep types separate for best results.

**Rubber**
- Location: Tires, hoses, gaskets, belts
- Types:
  - Natural rubber: Older materials, more flexible
  - Synthetic rubber: More durable, fuel-resistant
- Uses: Seals, gaskets, insulation, belts for power transmission
- Note: Degrades over time but remains usable for decades if stored properly.

**Lead**
- Location: Batteries (car, backup power), roofing, old pipes, wheel weights, radiation shielding
- Why important: Batteries (electricity storage), radiation shielding, soft solder, low melting point
- **WARNING**: Highly toxic. Wear gloves. Never heat without ventilation. Wash thoroughly after handling.

**Zinc**
- Location: Galvanized steel (coating), batteries, die-cast parts
- Uses: Corrosion protection, battery electrodes, alloy component (brass = copper + zinc)

### Tier 3: Specialized Components (Salvage Intact When Possible)

**Electronic Components**
Even if you can't manufacture integrated circuits yet, salvage them:
- **Microchips and ICs**: Every computer, phone, modern appliance. May still function. Store in anti-static bags.
- **LEDs**: Indicator lights everywhere. Long-lasting, efficient light sources.
- **Transistors**: Modern electronics have billions. Discrete transistors in older equipment.
- **Capacitors**: Power supplies, electronics. Large ones can store energy. Discharge before handling!
- **Resistors**: Color-coded components in all electronics. Sort by value.
- **Transformers**: Power adapters, electrical equipment. Convert voltage levels.
- **Relays**: Switching devices. Mechanical and solid-state types.

**Solar Panels**
- Location: Rooftops, solar farms, calculators, outdoor lights
- Durability: May function for 20-50 years if undamaged. Test with multimeter in sunlight.
- Priority: High. Free electricity with no fuel required.
- Extraction: Carefully remove glass-faced panels. Avoid cracking. Note voltage and current ratings.

**Batteries (If Not Leaked)**
- Lead-acid (car batteries): May be rechargeable if cells aren't damaged
- Lithium (phones, laptops): Dangerous if damaged. Handle carefully. May still hold charge.
- Alkaline (AA, AAA, etc.): Usually dead, but useful to dissect for chemicals
- Note: Battery recycling/refurbishment covered in Chapter 3.

**Motors and Generators**
- Location: Appliances, power tools, HVAC systems, vehicles, industrial equipment
- Why: Motors can be used as generators and vice versa. Contain copper wire, magnets, bearings.
- Types:
  - DC motors: Easier to control, reversible as generators
  - AC induction motors: More robust, common in appliances
  - Stepper motors: Precise positioning (printers, CNC machines)
- Keep intact if possible. Even damaged motors have valuable components.

**Precision Bearings**
- Location: Motors, car wheels, machine tools, hard drives
- Why: Extremely difficult to manufacture with precision. Enable rotating machinery.
- Types: Ball bearings, roller bearings, needle bearings
- Condition: Clean and oil. Even worn bearings are valuable.

**Optics**
- Camera lenses: Precision ground glass
- Binoculars and telescopes: Multiple lenses and prisms
- Microscopes: Essential for quality control in electronics
- Laser pointers: Contain laser diodes
- Fiber optic cables: Data transmission
- CD/DVD drives: Contain laser diodes, precision optics, and stepper motors

**Mechanical Components**
- Springs: Steel springs from vehicles, mattresses, industrial equipment
- Gears: Transmissions, machine tools, clocks
- Chains and sprockets: Bicycles, motorcycles, industrial equipment
- Belts and pulleys: V-belts from cars, timing belts
- Fasteners: Screws, bolts, nuts, rivets (sort by size and type)

**Tools and Measuring Instruments**
- Hand tools: Wrenches, screwdrivers, pliers, hammers
- Cutting tools: Saw blades, drill bits, files
- Measuring tools: Rulers, calipers, micrometers, levels
- Power tools: Drills, grinders, lathes, mills (may still function)
- Priority: Extremely high. These enable building everything else.

**Insulation Materials**
- Fiberglass: Building insulation, heat resistance
- Mineral wool: Fire-resistant insulation
- Ceramic materials: Heat tiles, kiln bricks, spark plug insulators
- Mica sheets: Electrical insulation, heat-resistant

## Geographic Salvage Priorities

### Urban Areas
**Advantages**: Dense concentration of materials, variety of buildings and equipment
**Disadvantages**: May be dangerous, contested, or radioactive (if weapons were used)

**Best salvage locations**:
1. **Suburbs**: Lower danger, still lots of materials in homes
2. **Industrial parks**: Machine shops, electronics manufacturers, warehouses
3. **Commercial areas**: Offices have copper wire, computers, HVAC systems
4. **Scrapyards**: Pre-sorted materials, though may be picked over
5. **Landfills**: Messy but enormous material reserves

### Rural Areas
**Advantages**: Agricultural equipment, spread-out buildings, often less damaged
**Disadvantages**: Lower material density, more travel needed

**Best salvage locations**:
1. **Farms**: Tractors, tools, diesel generators, electric motors, fuel tanks
2. **Small towns**: Hardware stores, garages, grain elevators
3. **Mines**: Equipment, explosives (careful!), processed ores
4. **Power infrastructure**: Transformers, transmission lines (dangerous voltage may remain!)

### Coastal Areas
**Advantages**: Shipping ports, naval facilities, maritime equipment
**Disadvantages**: Salt corrosion, potential flooding

**Best salvage locations**:
1. **Ports**: Cranes, containers (good storage), ships
2. **Boats**: Aluminum and fiberglass hulls, marine electronics, batteries
3. **Desalination plants**: Pumps, filters, industrial equipment

### Specialized Facilities (If Accessible)

**Airports**
- Aircraft: Aluminum alloys, titanium, precision instruments, radios, turbine blades
- Ground equipment: Generators, fuel pumps, tools
- Note: Some aircraft parts may be classified or hazardous (composites, exotic materials)

**Hospitals**
- Medical equipment: Stainless steel, precision instruments, computers, backup generators
- Laboratories: Glassware, chemicals (may still be usable), microscopes
- **WARNING**: Biological hazards, radioactive materials in imaging equipment, pharmaceutical chemicals

**Universities and Research Labs**
- High-quality instruments: Oscilloscopes, spectrometers, precision balances
- Computers and servers: May be more powerful than consumer equipment
- Libraries: Books and documentation (preserve these!)
- Machine shops: Lathes, mills, precision tools

**Data Centers**
- Servers: Dense concentration of electronics, copper, gold
- Batteries: Backup power systems, often large battery banks
- Cooling: Copper piping, pumps, heat exchangers
- Fiber optics: High-bandwidth cables

**Telecommunications Facilities**
- Cell towers: Radios, antennas, backup power systems
- Central offices: Switching equipment, fiber optics, copper lines
- Satellite dishes: Parabolic reflectors (useful for solar concentrators)

## Salvage Strategies

### Documentation
Before you take anything, document:
1. **Location**: Map it. You may need to return.
2. **Quantity**: Estimate available materials.
3. **Hazards**: Note dangers for future teams.
4. **Access**: How to get equipment in and out.

Create a salvage catalog:
```
Location: 123 Main Street, Commercial Building
Date Surveyed: [Date]
Structure Condition: Stable, west wall damaged
Hazards: Asbestos in ceiling tiles (2nd floor), mold in basement
Priority Materials:
  - Copper wire: ~50 kg (estimated) in walls and ceiling
  - Computers: 12 desktop PCs, various ages
  - HVAC system: 2 large units on roof, copper coils intact
  - Solar panels: 20 panels on roof, condition unknown
Access: Loading dock on north side, clear
Notes: Bring wire strippers, hand trucks, containers for components
```

### Systematic Extraction

**For a Building**:
1. **Exterior first**: Solar panels, HVAC units, external wiring, lightning rods
2. **Roof**: Flashing (metal), vents, additional HVAC
3. **Utilities**: Electrical panel, water heater, furnace, plumbing
4. **High-value rooms**:
   - Server rooms: Computers, networking equipment
   - Workshops: Tools, materials
   - Kitchens: Appliances, copper pipes, stainless steel
5. **Systematic stripping**: Work floor by floor, room by room
6. **Structural**: After all else is salvaged, consider rebar, structural steel

**For a Vehicle**:
1. **Battery**: May still be functional. Careful of acid.
2. **Alternator**: Generates electricity, contains copper and magnets.
3. **Starter motor**: Strong DC motor.
4. **Radiator**: Copper and aluminum, lots of it.
5. **Catalytic converter**: Contains platinum, palladium, rhodium.
6. **Wiring harness**: Hundreds of meters of copper wire.
7. **Computer modules**: Engine control unit, body control modules.
8. **Sensors**: Oxygen sensors, temperature sensors, etc.
9. **Lights**: LEDs in newer vehicles, bulbs in older ones.
10. **Body panels**: Aluminum or steel, depending on vehicle.
11. **Glass**: Windows, mirrors.
12. **Tires**: Rubber for various uses.

### Storage and Organization

Salvaged materials must be stored properly:

**Metals**:
- Sort by type: copper, aluminum, steel, stainless, brass, etc.
- Further sort by form: wire, sheet, structural, fasteners
- Storage: Keep dry to prevent corrosion. Oil steel to prevent rust.
- Label everything: "Copper Wire, 12-gauge, 15 kg, from 123 Main St."

**Electronics**:
- Anti-static: Store ICs and circuit boards in anti-static bags or wrap in aluminum foil
- Dry: Moisture causes corrosion
- Organized: Sort by type (capacitors, resistors, ICs, etc.)
- Documented: Note what device it came from if possible

**Glass**:
- Wrapped: Newspaper, cloth, or cardboard
- Separated: Don't let pieces touch
- Sorted: Laboratory glass separate from window glass
- Vertical storage: Less likely to break

**Chemicals and Batteries**:
- Ventilated: Fumes can build up
- Separate: Acids away from bases, flammables isolated
- Labeled: Clearly mark contents and hazards
- Contained: Double-contain anything that could leak
- Cool and dry: Extends shelf life

**Tools and Instruments**:
- Clean: Remove rust, oil moving parts
- Protected: Cases or wrapping to prevent damage
- Organized: Tool storage where items can be found quickly
- Maintained: Sharpen cutting tools, calibrate measuring instruments if possible

## Initial Salvage Priorities for a Starting Community

If you're just beginning, focus on this order:

**Week 1: Immediate Needs**
1. Hand tools (you need these to salvage everything else)
2. Containers (storage for salvage)
3. Solar panels and batteries (power for light and small tools)
4. Water containers and pipes
5. Wire and cable (copper)

**Month 1: Foundation Building**
1. More copper wire (hundreds of kg)
2. Steel and iron (structural materials)
3. Power tools (if you have electricity to run them)
4. Vehicles for transport (even if not running, can be repaired)
5. Machine tools (lathes, mills, drills)

**Year 1: Technology Base**
1. All electronic components you can find
2. Magnets, motors, and generators
3. Precision instruments and optics
4. Specialized materials (ceramics, refractories, insulation)
5. Chemical stockpiles (acids, bases, solvents)
6. Complete systems: computers, radios, test equipment

## Testing and Quality Control

Not all salvaged materials are usable:

**Metals**:
- Visual: Cracks, deep corrosion, or deformation may indicate weakness
- Magnet test: Distinguishes ferrous (magnetic) from non-ferrous metals
- Spark test: Different metals produce different sparks when ground (requires experience)
- File test: Hardness and texture indicate metal type
- Density: Weight vs. size can identify metals (gold is very heavy for its size)

**Electronics**:
- Visual: Look for burns, bulging capacitors, cracked components
- Multimeter: Test resistors, capacitors, diodes
- Power test: Carefully apply power to circuits, monitor for smoke or heat
- Note: Many components will still be good even if the device doesn't work

**Batteries**:
- Voltage test: Should be near rated voltage if good
- Load test: Connect to load, measure voltage drop
- Physical: Swelling, leaking, or damage means discard or recycle
- Lead-acid: Check electrolyte level and specific gravity if possible

**Wire and Cable**:
- Insulation: Cracked or brittle insulation should be stripped or replaced
- Conductor: Verify copper vs. aluminum (copper is more valuable)
- Corrosion: Green (copper) or white (aluminum) oxidation. Can be cleaned.

## Long-Term Salvage Consideration

**The salvage won't last forever.** Eventually, you'll have picked over the ruins. This is why manufacturing capability is critical. Use the initial salvage period (probably decades) to:

1. Build manufacturing capability while materials are available
2. Prioritize hard-to-make items (precision instruments, complex electronics)
3. Recycle and reuse everything possible
4. Establish mining and refining for post-salvage era
